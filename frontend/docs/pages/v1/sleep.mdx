# Sleep & Delays

When a task needs to pause, Hatchet can free the worker slot for the duration of the wait — whether that is a few seconds or a few weeks. No resources are held while the task is sleeping.

<LongWaitDiagram />

Both durable tasks and DAGs support sleeping, but the model differs: durable tasks issue sleep waits dynamically at runtime, while DAGs declare sleep conditions upfront on the task definition.

## Durable sleep

With a regular language-level sleep — `time.sleep` in Python, `setTimeout` in Node — the wait is tied to the local process. If the process restarts mid-sleep, the clock resets and you end up sleeping the full duration again. Sleep for 24 hours, restart after 23, and you've waited 47 hours total.

Durable sleep tracks the original deadline server-side. If a task sleeping for 24 hours is interrupted after 23, it only sleeps for 1 more hour on restart. The deadline is respected across interruptions.

<Callout type="info">
  Sleeping puts the task into an [evictable state](/v1/task-eviction) — the
  worker slot is freed and the task is re-queued when the sleep expires.
</Callout>

## Sleep conditions

Sleep conditions pause a DAG task for a specified duration before it runs. Use them when a task should wait for a fixed amount of time after its parent tasks complete.

Unlike durable sleep (which is triggered dynamically at runtime), DAG sleep conditions are declared upfront on the task definition. Both free the worker slot during the wait. See [Worker Slots & Waiting](/v1/task-eviction) for details.

### Using sleep conditions

Declare a task with a `wait_for` sleep condition. The task will wait for its parents to complete, then sleep for the specified duration before executing.

### Combining with other conditions

Sleep conditions can be combined with other conditions using or groups. For example, you can wait for _either_ a sleep duration or an event — whichever comes first. See [Conditions & Branching](/v1/conditions#or-groups) for details.
